Most people know AI is "getting better fast," but few can visualize the pace. The METR paper introduces the **time horizon** — the length of task (measured in human work time) that AI can complete autonomously with 50% reliability. This gives a single, intuitive, jaw-dropping metric:

- **6 years ago:** AI could handle tasks that take humans **~2 minutes**
- **Today (Claude Sonnet 3.7):** AI can handle tasks that take humans **~1 hour**
- **The trend:** Doubling every **~7 months**
- **Extrapolation to decade's end:** AI capable of **month-long autonomous projects**

This isn't a benchmark score (who remembers if a model scored 88.6% or 89.2%?). It's a visceral number — *"an AI agent in 2030 will handle what takes you a month."*

The paper also shows that models today succeed **~100% on tasks under 4 minutes**, but drop below **10% on tasks over 4 hours** — which neatly explains why AI feels magical for quick chores and comically bad at "real work." The bottleneck isn't intelligence — it's *duration of reliable execution*.
